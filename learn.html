Therapy is all about cooking up a better mindset. It‚Äôs all about breaking down complex thoughts and feelings into manageable pieces, then carefully mixing in new perspectives to change the recipe of how someone sees the world.

A therapist helps you find the right formula to turn emotional disturbances into clarity, guiding you through the reaction of self-concept and growth without letting things blow up.
In the world of psychology, there are different 'labs' of therapy, each with its own special method for transforming mental states. Some focus on the deep, hidden ingredients from past experiences, while others tweak the current thought processes to create a cleaner, stronger mindset. Whatever the approach, therapy‚Äôs ultimate goal is to help you live a life that‚Äôs healthier, more balanced, and way less volatile.

In AP Psychology, you study psychotherapies, biological treatments, and methods used by therapists. The unit also covers the historical development of therapeutic approaches, the effectiveness of therapy and ethical considerations in therapeutic practice.

Psychoanalysis is a way of understanding ourselves that was started by Sigmund Freud. He believed that our thoughts, feelings, and actions are often shaped by things deep inside our minds that we‚Äôre not fully aware of, especially from childhood. By talking openly and thinking deeply about our past and emotions, we can discover hidden reasons for how we feel or act. This can help people deal with emotional pain, change unhelpful habits, and understand themselves better.

  • Free Association: The person in therapy talks freely about whatever comes to mind including thoughts, memories, feelings, without trying to filter or organize them. This helps bring hidden thoughts and emotions to the surface.
  • Dream Analysis: Dreams are explored to find both the obvious content (what happens in the dream) and the hidden meaning underneath. Freud believed dreams give clues to unconscious thoughts and desires.
  • Transference: The person in therapy may start to direct feelings they have toward important people in their life (like parents or partners) onto the therapist. This helps uncover unresolved emotions and relationship patterns.
  • Resistance: Sometimes, people avoid or struggle to talk about certain topics. This ‚Äúresistance‚Äù can show where painful or deeply buried emotions are, giving clues to what needs to be explored.

  • Unconditional Positive Regard: Therapist offers acceptance and non-judgment.

  • Systematic Desensitization: Gradual exposure to feared stimulus while teaching relaxation. Also includes Virtual Reality
  • Counterconditioning: Replacing an unwanted response with a positive one by pairing the trigger with something pleasant. (e.g. pairing nausea drugs with alcohol to prevent the use of it).
  • Aversive Conditioning: Reducing unwanted behavior by pairing it with something unpleasant or uncomfortable.
  • Flooding: Exposing someone to their fear all at once until the anxiety fades.
  • VR Exposure Therapy: Simulating environments to safely expose clients to a feared stimuli, helping reduce anxiety.
  • Token Economy: Rewards (tokens) exchanged for privileges to reinforce positive behavior.
  • Reinforcements and Punishments: Reinforce desired behaviors, punish undesired behaviors.

CBT is a therapy that helps people identify and change negative thought patterns and behaviors to improve how they feel and cope with problems.

Effective for depression, anxiety, OCD.

A therapy that helps people identify irrational beliefs, challenge them, and replace them with more rational, helpful thoughts to reduce emotional distress.

Medications are used to treat psychological disorders by altering brain chemistry.

  • SSRIs: Increase serotonin by blocking reuptake (e.g., Prozac, Zoloft).
  • Benzodiazepines: Fast-acting CNS depressants (e.g., Xanax), habit-forming.
  • First-Gen (Typical): Block dopamine receptors (e.g., Thorazine), side effects include tardive dyskinesia.
  • Second-Gen (Atypical): Target dopamine & serotonin (e.g., Risperdal), fewer motor side effects.
  • Lithium: Stabilizes bipolar mood swings, requires monitoring.

Methylphenidate and Amphetamines increase dopamine and norepinephrine to improve focus.

Therapy has been shown to be effective for many mental health issues, including depression, anxiety, and trauma. Studies show that most people who go to therapy improve over time. Different types of therapy work better for different problems (e.g., CBT for anxiety). The relationship between therapist and client plays a big role in success. Overall, therapy can lead to better emotional well-being, coping skills, and life satisfaction.

Eclectic Therapy: Therapists often combine approaches to fit individual needs.

  • Confidentiality: Client info is private with few exceptions.
  • Informed Consent: Clients must understand and agree to therapy nature.
  • Nonmaleficence: Protect clients from harm.
  • Honour and Integrity: Be truthful about qualifications, treatment, and progress.

Historical practices now considered harmful or outdated:

  • Beating: Physical punishment to 'cure' mental illness.
  • Bloodletting: Draining blood to balance bodily humors.
  • Exorcism: Attempting to drive out evil spirits.
  • Isolation: Confining patients in asylums.
  • Lobotomy: Severing brain connections (already discussed).
  • Other Rituals: Various unscientific, often cruel treatments.
  • Psychotherapy: Psychological treatment designed to help individuals overcome emotional, behavioral, or mental health challenges by interacting with a trained therapist.
  • Biomedical Therapy: Treatment involving medical procedures or medications to address psychological disorders, often combined with psychotherapy.
  • Eclectic Approach: A flexible therapeutic style combining techniques from multiple therapies tailored to the client's unique needs.
  • Psychoanalysis: Freud‚Äôs method focusing on unconscious childhood influences to understand and resolve emotional issues.
  • Resistance: In psychoanalysis, unconscious defense mechanisms preventing revealing sensitive thoughts or feelings.
  • Transference: Redirection of feelings for one person onto the therapist, a key concept in psychoanalysis.
  • Free Association: Speaking freely to reveal hidden thoughts and feelings.
  • Dream Analysis: Examining dreams to uncover unconscious desires and thoughts.
  • Humanistic Therapies: Focus on personal growth and self-healing through acceptance and empathy.
  • Client-Centered Therapy: Carl Rogers‚Äô approach emphasizing unconditional positive regard, empathy, and genuineness.
  • Hierarchy of Needs: Maslow‚Äôs theory that basic needs must be met before self-actualization.
  • Behavioral Therapies: Using conditioning techniques to change behavior.
  • Systematic Desensitization: Gradually exposing clients to feared stimuli while teaching relaxation to reduce anxiety.
  • Counterconditioning: Replacing unwanted responses with positive ones by pairing triggers with pleasant stimuli.
  • Operant Conditioning: Using reinforcements and punishments to modify behavior.
  • Token Economy: Behavioral modification system where clients earn tokens for desired behaviors exchangeable for rewards.
  • Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Therapy combining cognitive and behavioral techniques to change negative thought patterns and behaviors.
  • Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT): Therapy challenging irrational beliefs to reduce emotional distress.
  • Psychopharmacology: Study and use of medications to treat psychological disorders.
  • Antidepressants (SSRIs): Medications that increase serotonin levels to treat depression.
  • Antianxiety Medications: Drugs like benzodiazepines that reduce anxiety symptoms.
  • Antipsychotic Medications: Medications blocking dopamine receptors to treat psychosis.
  • Mood Stabilizers: Drugs such as lithium used to control mood swings, especially in bipolar disorder.
  • Stimulants (for ADHD): Medications that increase dopamine and norepinephrine to improve focus.
  • Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT): Electrical stimulation causing seizures to treat severe depression.
  • Psychosurgery: Surgical procedures targeting brain areas to alleviate psychological disorders (e.g., lobotomy).
  • Group Therapy: Therapy involving multiple clients sharing experiences and support.
  • Family Therapy: Therapy improving communication and resolving conflicts within families.
  • Effectiveness of Therapy: Therapy improves mental health by enhancing emotional well-being and coping skills.
  • Active Listening: Communication technique involving full attention, understanding, and feedback, often used in humanistic therapies.
